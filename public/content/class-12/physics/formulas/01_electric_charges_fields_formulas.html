1.1

Electric Charge — Basic Properties

Charge is a scalar property of matter. Three defining properties are tested repeatedly: quantisation, additivity and conservation.

Quantisation of Charge GREEN
q = n e
n = 0, ±1, ±2, ±3 …; e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C (elementary charge). Charge on any body is always an integral multiple of e — it can never be a fraction of e (except quarks, outside CBSE scope).
Additivity of Charge GREEN
Q = q₁ + q₂ + q₃ + … + qₙ
Total/net charge of a system is the algebraic sum (signs included) of individual charges — not vector addition, since charge is a scalar.
Conservation of Charge RED
Total charge of an isolated system = constant
Charge can neither be created nor destroyed; it only transfers between bodies. Used to justify every "charge transferred by friction/contact" numerical.
Electrons transferred (derivation step) DERIVED
n = qe   and   m = n × mₑ
Direct rearrangement of q = ne, combined with electron mass mₑ = 9 × 10⁻³¹ kg, to find mass transferred during charging (used in Ex. 1 & 2 of the book).
1.2

Coulomb's Law & Electrostatic Field

The single most important law of the chapter — scalar form, vector form, medium correction, and its comparison with gravitation.

Coulomb's Law (scalar, vacuum) RED
F = k |q₁q₂|r²
Force between two stationary point charges is directly proportional to the product of charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.
Constant k
k = 14πε₀ = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²C⁻²
Permittivity of free space
ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² C²N⁻¹m⁻²
Dimensional formula of ε₀
[M⁻¹L⁻³T⁴A²]
Coulomb's Law — Vector Form RED
𝑭₁₂ = 14πε₀ q₁q₂r² r̂₂₁
Force on q₁ due to q₂, directed along unit vector r̂₂₁ (pointing from q₂ to q₁).
Newton's third law check
𝑭₁₂ = − 𝑭₂₁   (since r̂₂₁ = − r̂₁₂)
One Coulomb — defining condition GREEN
F = 9 × 10⁹ N   when q₁=q₂=1 C, r = 1 m
1 C is the charge that repels an identical charge 1 m away with a force of 9 × 10⁹ N — an enormous, impractically large unit.
CGS unit of charge
esu or stat-coulomb: F = 1 dyne ⇒ q = ±1 stat-coulomb (r = 1 cm)
esu ↔ SI conversion GOLD — added
1 C = 3 × 10⁹ stat-coulomb (esu)
Not stated explicitly in the book but frequently needed to relate CGS and SI charge units in objective questions.
Force in a Dielectric Medium RED
Fmedium = 14πε |q₁q₂|r²
ε = absolute permittivity of the medium.
Dielectric constant / Relative permittivity K
K = εr = εε₀ = FvacuumFmedium   ⇒   ε = Kε₀
Comparison with Gravitation GREEN
FG = Gm₁m₂r²
G = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg². Both follow the inverse-square law; electrostatic force between an electron & proton is FE ≈ 10³⁹ FG — electrostatic force is far stronger.
Superposition Principle RED
𝑭₁ = 𝑭₁₂ + 𝑭₁₃ + … + 𝑭₁ₙ = 14πε₀ Σ q₁qᵢr₁ᵢ² r̂ᵢ₁
Net force on a charge due to a system of charges is the vector sum of forces due to each individual charge, unaffected by presence of other charges.
1.3

Electrostatic Force due to Continuous Charge Distribution

Charge densities convert a discrete sum into an integral — the bridge concept to Gauss's law applications in 1.9.

Linear Charge Density GREEN
λ = dqdl  ⇒  dq = λ dl
SI unit: C/m. Used for wires/rods.
Force
𝑭 = q₀4πε₀ ∫ λ dlr₀² r̂₀
Surface Charge Density GREEN
σ = dqdS  ⇒  dq = σ dS
SI unit: C/m². Used for plates/sheets/spherical shells.
Force
𝑭 = q₀4πε₀ ∫ σ dSr₀² r̂₀
Volume Charge Density GREEN
ρ = dqdV  ⇒  dq = ρ dV
SI unit: C/m³. Used for solid charged spheres.
Force
𝑭 = q₀4πε₀ ∫ ρ dVr₀² r̂₀
Volume charge density from total Q (derivation step) DERIVED
ρ = q(4/3)πR³
Sub-formula obtained by substituting V = (4/3)πR³ of a sphere into ρ = q/V — used to find volume charge density of a uniformly charged sphere (e.g. gold nucleus example).
Surface density from total charge (derivation step) DERIVED
σ = q4πr²
σ = q/S with S = 4πr² for a sphere — the sub-step used to electrify a sphere to a given surface charge density.
1.4

Electric Field

Field = force experienced per unit (vanishingly small) test charge.

Definition of Electric Field RED
𝑬 = 𝑭q₀ = limq₀→0 𝑭q₀
Vector quantity; SI unit NC⁻¹ or Vm⁻¹. Dimensional formula [MLT⁻³A⁻¹]. Test charge q₀ must be vanishingly small so it does not disturb the source charge's own field.
Field due to a Point Charge RED
𝑬 = 14πε₀ qr² r̂
Magnitude E = q/(4πε₀r²) — direction away from +q, towards −q. Field has spherical symmetry (same magnitude at all points equidistant from the charge).
Field due to a System of Charges RED
𝑬 = 𝑬₁+𝑬₂+…+𝑬ₙ = 14πε₀ Σ qᵢrᵢ² r̂ᵢ
Principle of superposition applied to fields — used for two-charge, three-charge and ring/line configurations.
Electric field on the axis of a uniformly charged ring GOLD — from Ch.1.4 fig, formalised
E = qr4πε₀(r²+a²)3/2
q = total ring charge, a = radius of ring, r = distance of point from centre along the axis. Direction is along the axis (OX). Maximum E occurs at r = a/√2 (standard NCERT result — add to memory for MCQs).
1.5

Electric Field Lines

Purely conceptual/geometric section — no numeric formula, but the properties below are tested as assertion-reason & conceptual questions every year.

Properties to remember (green-tier recall list):
  1. Field lines start from +charge, end on −charge (or infinity).
  2. Two field lines never intersect (field can't have two directions at one point).
  3. In a charge-free region, field lines are continuous curves without breaks.
  4. Field lines do not form closed loops (electrostatic field is conservative).
  5. Field lines are perpendicular to the surface of a charged conductor.
  6. Field lines contract lengthwise to represent attraction; exert sideways pressure to represent repulsion.
  7. Density of field lines ∝ magnitude of E (crowded near charge, sparse far away).
  8. Field lines never pass through a conductor — interior of a conductor is field-free.
1.6

Electric Dipole

Every dipole question needs the exact formula AND its short-dipole (l ≪ r) approximation — both are given here.

Dipole Moment RED
𝒑 = q × (2𝒍)
Direction: from −q to +q. |p| = q(2l). SI unit: C·m.
Dimensional formula GOLD
[M⁰L¹T¹A¹] — not printed in book, standard CBSE requirement.
Field on the Axial Line (exact) RED
Eaxial = 14πε₀ 2px(x²−l²)²
Short-dipole approximation (l ≪ x)
Eaxial = 14πε₀ 2px³
Direction: along BX produced, same direction as p.
Field on the Equatorial Line (exact) RED
Eequatorial = 14πε₀ p(x²+l²)3/2
Short-dipole approximation (l ≪ x)
Eequatorial = 14πε₀ px³
Direction: antiparallel to p (parallel to BA, opposite to axis direction).
Golden Ratio Result RED — always tested
Eaxial = 2 Eequatorial
At the same distance from the dipole centre, for a short dipole. One of the most frequently asked one-mark facts.
Field at a General Point (angle θ from axis) RED
E = 14πε₀ pr³ √(3cos²θ + 1)
Angle α between resultant E and the dipole axis
tan α = 12 tan θ
Special cases below reduce this general formula to the axial & equatorial results.
Case reductions of the general formula (derivation steps) DERIVED
θ = 0° (axial): E = (1/4πε₀)(2p/r³)
θ = 90° (equatorial): E = (1/4πε₀)(p/r³)
1.7

Dipole in a Uniform External Field

Torque, work and potential energy — all three are direct 2–3 mark derivation questions.

Torque on a Dipole RED
τ = pE sin θ  |  𝛕 = 𝒑 × 𝑬
θ = angle between p and E. Net force on dipole in a uniform field is zero (F and −F act at different points ⇒ pure couple).
Work Done in Rotating a Dipole GREEN
dW = τ dθ = pE sin θ dθ
Integrated (θ₁ → θ₂)
W = pE(cos θ₁ − cos θ₂)
Potential Energy of a Dipole RED
U = −pE cos θ = −𝒑 · 𝑬
Taken as work done from θ₁ = 90° (U = 0 reference) to θ.
Special Equilibrium Cases GOLD — tabulated for clarity

1.8

Electric Flux & Gauss's Theorem

Flux is the geometric bridge that converts field calculations into simple algebra via Gauss's law.

Elementary Flux (derivation step) DERIVED
dφ = 𝑬 · d𝑺 = E dS cos θ
θ = angle between E and the area-vector normal n̂. This is integrated to obtain total flux below.
Total Electric Flux RED
φ = ∮ 𝑬 · d𝑺 = ∮ E dS cos θ
SI unit: N·m²C⁻¹ or volt-metre. Dimensional formula [ML³T⁻³A⁻¹]. Scalar quantity, but is a property of the vector field E.
Sign of Flux — Special Angles GREEN
0° < θ < 90° → φ positive  |  θ = 90° → φ = 0  |  90° < θ < 180° → φ negative
Gauss's Theorem RED — most important law in chapter
φE = ∮ 𝑬 · d𝑺 = qε₀
q = Σqᵢ = total (net) charge enclosed by the Gaussian surface. Surface integral of E over any closed surface in free space equals (1/ε₀) × charge enclosed.
Zero-flux corollary
If qenclosed = 0 ⇒ φ = 0, even if there are charges outside the surface — external charges contribute zero net flux (though they do affect the local E at points on the surface).
Deduction of Coulomb's Law from Gauss's Theorem (derivation) DERIVED
dφ = (1/4πε₀)(q/r²) dS  →  E(4πr²) = q/ε₀  →  E = q/(4πε₀r²)  →  F = qq₀/(4πε₀r²)
1.9

Applications of Gauss's Theorem

Four standard symmetric-charge configurations — each is a guaranteed 3–5 mark derivation.

Infinitely Long Straight Charged Wire RED
E = λ2πε₀r
Vector form: 𝑬 = λ/(2πε₀r) r̂. Gaussian surface: cylinder of radius r, length l. Field radial, ⊥ to wire; flux through flat end-caps = 0.
Derivation step
E(2πrl) = λl/ε₀
Infinite Thin Plane Sheet of Charge RED
E = σ2ε₀
Field is uniform, independent of distance from the sheet, directed away from sheet (if σ>0) on both sides. Gaussian surface: cylindrical "pillbox" straddling the sheet.
Derivation step
2EA = σA/ε₀
Uniformly Charged Non-Conducting Solid Sphere RED
Outside (r > R): E = q/(4πε₀r²)
Inside (r < R): E = ρr/(3ε₀)
At centre (r = 0): E = 0
At surface (r = R): E = Rρ/(3ε₀)  (maximum value)
Uniformly Charged Conducting Sphere / Spherical Shell GOLD — not in given pages, standard CBSE
Outside (r > R): E = q/(4πε₀r²) = kq/r²
On the surface (r = R): E = q/(4πε₀R²) = σ/ε₀
Inside (r < R): E = 0 (all charge resides on the outer surface of a conductor)
Field due to a charged conductor's surface GOLD — standard result
E = σε₀
Just outside any charged conductor's surface (not σ/2ε₀ as for an isolated sheet), because the field inside a conductor is zero and all flux emerges outward through the Gaussian pillbox's outer face only.
Field between two oppositely charged parallel plates GOLD — capacitor lead-in, not in given pages
Ebetween = σε₀  ,  Eoutside = 0
Superposition of two sheets (+σ and −σ) each contributing σ/2ε₀. Fields add between the plates, cancel outside. Foundation for the parallel-plate capacitor (next chapter) — frequently asked as an extension question here.
Field between two identically (same-sign) charged parallel plates GOLD — extension case
Ebetween = 0  ,  Eoutside = σε₀
Fields cancel between (equal, opposite-direction contributions) and add outside — the reverse of the opposite-charge case above. A favourite assertion-reason trap.

Hypothetical "what-if" conceptual traps (frequently examined)
  • If field lines could intersect, the field would have two directions at that point — physically impossible, hence they never do.
  • If Gauss's law is applied to an irregular (non-symmetric) surface, it is still valid — but E cannot be pulled out of the integral, so it becomes practically unsolvable without symmetry.
  • If a dipole is cut in half through its centre (perpendicular to axis), each half is not a dipole of moment p/2 — it becomes two separate single point charges, since dipole moment depends on the product of q and separation as a whole entity.
  • If ε₀ were doubled (hypothetically), Coulomb force and E for a given charge would both halve — used to test dimensional/relational understanding, not a real scenario.
  • A neutral atom in an external field still experiences a net torque-inducing "induced dipole" effect — not zero force, because the field can polarise it (beyond CBSE numerically, but conceptually asked).
