Foundation

Magnetic Flux (Φ)

Magnetic flux is the total number of magnetic field lines passing through a surface normally. Think of it as how much of the magnetic field is "captured" by the loop.

Magnetic Field
Uniform field (Tesla)

Magnetic Flux Formula
Φ = B · A = BA cosθ
θ = angle between B and area normal â. Unit: Weber (Wb). 1 Wb = 1 T·m².
Case I: θ=90° → Φ=0 (B parallel to surface, no lines cross through it)
Case II: θ=0° → Φ=BA (maximum — B perpendicular to surface)
Key insight: It's not the field strength alone that matters — it's how much field passes THROUGH the loop area. Rotating the loop changes flux even if B doesn't change!

Core Principle

Faraday's Laws of Electromagnetic Induction

1️⃣
Faraday's First Law
Whenever the amount of magnetic flux linked with a circuit changes, an EMF and current is induced. It lasts as long as the change continues.
2️⃣
Faraday's Second Law
The magnitude of induced EMF equals the rate of change of magnetic flux linked with the circuit.
Faraday's Second Law — Induced EMF
e = − dΦ_B / dt
Negative sign = Lenz's law (EMF opposes change). For N turns: e = −N dΦ_B/dt
For N-turn coil
e = −N · dΦ_B/dt
More turns → more EMF. Each turn adds its contribution. This is why transformers have many turns.

Induced Current and Charge

Induced Current (closed circuit)
I = e/R = −(N/R) · dΦ_B/dt
Charge that flows (independent of time!)
q = N·ΔΦ/R
Note: q depends only on ΔΦ (change in flux), NOT on how fast the change happens. This is used to measure flux experimentally!

Direction of Induced Current

Lenz's Law — The Opposition Principle

Lenz's Law: The induced EMF is always in such a direction that it tends to produce a current which opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced it.
1
N-pole approaches coil → flux increases → induced current opposes

The front face of the coil becomes N-pole (repels the approaching magnet). Current flows anticlockwise when viewed from the magnet side.

2
N-pole moves away → flux decreases → induced current supports

The front face becomes S-pole (attracts the receding magnet). Current reverses to clockwise.

3
Why does Lenz's law obey conservation of energy?

You must do mechanical work to move the magnet against the opposing force. This work converts to electrical energy (induced current). If Lenz's law were reversed, we'd get energy for free — impossible!

Fleming's Right Hand Rule — Direction of induced current
Thumb → Motion · Forefinger → B · Middle → I
Stretch thumb, forefinger, middle finger mutually perpendicular. Thumb = direction of conductor motion, Forefinger = B field direction (N→S), Middle finger = direction of induced current.

EMF by Motion

Motional Electromotive Force (e = Blv)

When a conductor of length l moves with velocity v perpendicular to a magnetic field B, the free electrons in the conductor experience a magnetic force and move — creating an EMF.

1
Conductor PQ moves rightward with velocity v in field B (into page)

Consider loop PQRS. As PQ moves right by distance dy in time dt, the area swept = l·dy.

2
Change in flux = B × change in area
Flux change rate
dΦ/dt = B · l · (dy/dt) = Blv
3
By Faraday's law → Motional EMF
Motional EMF — Master Formula
e = Blv
B = field strength (T) · l = length of conductor (m) · v = velocity (m/s)
Valid when B, l, v are mutually perpendicular (all 90° to each other)
Direction: Use Fleming's Right Hand Rule. For B into page (⊗) and v rightward → current flows from Q to P (upward in wire).

Three Ways to Induce EMF

Methods of Producing Induced EMF

Since Φ = BA cosθ, there are exactly three ways to change flux and produce EMF:

🧲
Method 1: Change B
Change magnitude of field B while loop stays still. Used in: transformers, induction heaters.
📏
Method 2: Change A
Change the area of loop inside the field. Moving a conductor in field changes effective area. Used in: generators, rail-gun EMF.
🔄
Method 3: Change θ
Rotate the coil in a uniform field — angle θ between B and normal changes. Used in: AC generators, alternators.

AC Generator — Rotating Coil (Method 3)

EMF from rotating coil in uniform B
e = NBAω sinωt = e₀ sinωt
e₀ = NBAω = peak EMF · ω = angular velocity (rad/s) · N = turns · A = area · B = field
Induced current: I = I₀ sinωt where I₀ = e₀/R (peak current)
Why sinωt? When coil plane is parallel to B (θ=90° between normal and B), the flux change rate is maximum → peak EMF. When coil is perpendicular to B (normal ∥ B), flux is maximum but rate of change = 0 → zero EMF. These are 90° apart — hence sinusoidal output!

Self Induction

Self-Inductance — The Inertia of Electricity

Self-inductance is the property of a coil by which it opposes any change in current flowing through it by inducing a back EMF. It is electricity's version of inertia.

1
Flux ∝ Current

For N turns, NΦ_B ∝ I → NΦ_B = LI where L = self-inductance coefficient.

Definition of Self-Inductance
L = NΦ_B / I
L is numerically equal to flux linkage per unit current. Unit: Henry (H). 1H = 1 Vs/A = 1 Wb/A
2
Back EMF (opposing change in current)
Self-induced back EMF
e = −L · dI/dt
Negative sign = always opposes change. When current increases, e opposes increase. When current decreases, e tries to maintain it. This is why inductors resist sudden changes.
3
Self-Inductance of a Long Solenoid
Solenoid self-inductance
L = μ₀N²A/l  or  L = μ₀μᵣN²A/l (with core)
N = total turns · A = cross-section area · l = length · μᵣ = relative permeability of core
Iron core (μᵣ ≈ 1000) increases L by 1000×!
Definition of 1 Henry: A coil has self-inductance of 1 henry when a current changing at rate of 1 A/s induces an EMF of 1 volt in it. (If dI/dt = 1, then L = e numerically)

Mutual Inductance — Two Coupled Coils

Mutual Inductance M
M = μ₀N₁N₂A / l
EMF in secondary: e₂ = −M · dI₁/dt
M = mutual inductance (H). Depends on geometry and closeness of coils. Basis of transformers!
Coupled Coils

Mutual Induction — Transformer Principle

When current in one coil (primary) changes, it induces EMF in a nearby coil (secondary). This is mutual induction — the basis of every transformer.

EMF induced in secondary by primary
e₂ = −M · dI₁/dt
M = mutual inductance · dI₁/dt = rate of change of primary current
Larger M → more coupling → more EMF transferred

Relation M² = L₁L₂ (ideal coupling)

Coupling coefficient k
M = k√(L₁L₂)  where 0 ≤ k ≤ 1
k=1 = perfect coupling (all flux links both coils, like iron core transformer). k≈0 = loose coupling (air-core, coils far apart).

Energy Storage

Energy Stored in an Inductor

An inductor stores energy in its magnetic field, just as a capacitor stores energy in its electric field. This energy comes from the work done to establish the current against back-EMF.

1
Work done to move charge dq against back-EMF e

dW = −e·dq = L(dI/dt)·dq = L·I·dI  [since dq = I·dt]

2
Total work to build current from 0 to I
Energy stored in inductor
W = U = ½LI²
This is stored as magnetic field energy inside the inductor. Analogous to U = ½CV² for capacitor.
3
Energy density (energy per unit volume)
Magnetic energy density
u = U/V = B²/(2μ₀)
This is the energy stored per unit volume of the magnetic field. Compare with electric energy density: u_E = ε₀E²/2

Physical Meaning: 1 Henry

When I = 1A and L = 1H: U = ½ × 1 × 1² = 0.5 J. So 1 henry inductor carrying 1 ampere stores 0.5 joules. This is why large inductors in power systems carry enormous energy that must be handled carefully when switching off.

⚠️ Common Exam Mistakes

Mistake 1: Ignoring the negative sign in e = −dΦ/dt ▼
The negative sign is Lenz's law mathematically encoded. It tells you the induced EMF OPPOSES the change. In numerical problems, we use magnitude |e| = dΦ/dt. But for direction questions, the negative sign tells you to use Lenz's law to find current direction.
Mistake 2: θ in Φ = BA cosθ is measured from wrong reference ▼
θ is the angle between B and the area normal (â) — NOT between B and the loop surface. When B is perpendicular to loop surface (parallel to normal), θ=0°, cosθ=1, Φ=max. When B is parallel to loop surface (perpendicular to normal), θ=90°, cosθ=0, Φ=0.
Mistake 3: Confusing L = NΦ/I with e = −L·dI/dt ▼
L = NΦ/I defines what L IS (ratio of flux linkage to current). The back EMF formula e = −L·dI/dt tells you what L DOES (produces back EMF when current changes). Both are needed — don't mix them up. L doesn't change with I (for linear inductors).
Mistake 4: Charge q = NΔΦ/R is time-independent — students think it depends on how fast ▼
q = ∫I dt = ∫(e/R)dt = (1/R)∫e dt = (N/R)∫dΦ = NΔΦ/R. The integral of dΦ/dt over time is just ΔΦ — time cancels out! So the same amount of charge flows whether you move the magnet fast or slow, as long as the total flux change ΔΦ is the same.
Mistake 5: Motional EMF needs all three (B, l, v) to be perpendicular to each other ▼
e = Blv only when B ⊥ l ⊥ v (all mutually perpendicular). If B is at angle to v, use e = Blv sinθ where θ is angle between B and v. If conductor makes angle with perpendicular, use the component of l perpendicular to v. Always check geometry first!
