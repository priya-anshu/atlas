Foundation

Electron Emission

›

Free electrons in metals are bound by a surface barrier. Minimum energy to escape = Work Function $(\phi_0)$ measured in eV.

🔥 Thermionic Emission

Heating provides thermal energy. Used in CRT tubes, spark plugs. Emitted electrons = thermions.

⚡ Field Emission

Very strong E-field (~$10^8$ V/m) pulls electrons out. No heating needed.

💡 Photoelectric Emission

Light of suitable frequency → electron emission. Depends on frequency, not intensity.

☢️ Secondary Emission

High-energy primary electrons hit metal → secondary electrons ejected.

Work Function Key Points

  • 1 eV $= 1.6\times10^{-19}$ J
  • Platinum: highest $\phi_0 = 5.65$ eV · Caesium: lowest $\phi_0 = 2.14$ eV
  • Alkali metals (Li, Na, K, Cs) → photoelectric with visible light
  • Zn, Cd, Mg → need UV light · Non-metals: sensitive only up to limited extent

11.1

Photoelectric Effect — Experimental Study

›

Hertz (1887)

Discovered EM waves. Noted UV light on negative spark-gap plate increases sparking — could not explain why.

Hallwachs & Lenard (1886–1902)

  • UV on zinc plate → electrons emitted (current flows)
  • Negative emitter plate loses negative charge → confirms emission
  • Lenard showed: if collector is negative, current stops

Effect of Potential on Photoelectric Current

📈

Accelerating Potential ↑

Current increases until it reaches saturation. Beyond saturation, no change.

🔋

Retarding Potential

Current decreases but is non-zero at V=0 (electrons have own KE). Becomes 0 at $-V_0$.

🛑

Stopping Potential $V_0$

Minimum negative collector voltage that stops all electrons. $eV_0 = K_{max}$

Effect of Intensity

  • More intensity → more photoelectrons per second → higher saturation current
  • Stopping potential is unchanged with intensity
  • Photoelectric current ∝ intensity of incident light

⚡ Instantaneous Emission

Time lag < $10^{-9}$ s even for very dim light — wave theory cannot explain this.

Why Wave Theory Fails

  • Wave theory: emission for any frequency if intensity is high enough → contradicted by threshold frequency
  • Wave theory: time lag for energy accumulation → contradicted by instantaneous emission
  • Wave theory: KE should increase with intensity → contradicted (KE depends only on ν)
  • No concept of threshold frequency in wave theory

Einstein (1905)

Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

›

Light consists of discrete energy packets called photons. One photon interacts with one electron.

Photon Energy

$$E = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

$h = 6.63\times10^{-34}$ J·s (Planck's constant)

Energy use by photon hitting metal:

  • Part 1: $\phi_0 = h\nu_0$ — overcomes work function (surface barrier)
  • Part 2: Remaining energy → $K_{max}$ of emitted photoelectron

Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

$$K_{max} = h\nu - \phi_0 = h(\nu-\nu_0)$$
$$\frac{1}{2}mv_{max}^2 = h\nu - h\nu_0$$
$$eV_0 = h(\nu - \nu_0)$$

In terms of wavelength

$$eV_0 = hc\!\left(\frac{1}{\lambda}-\frac{1}{\lambda_0}\right)$$

Photon Momentum

$$p = \frac{h}{\lambda} = \frac{h\nu}{c}$$

Non-zero despite zero rest mass!

📜
Laws

Laws of Photoelectric Emission

›
1

Current ∝ Intensity (fixed ν). More photons → more electrons per second.

2

Saturation current ∝ intensity; stopping potential is independent of intensity.

3

Threshold frequency $\nu_0$: below this, no emission. Characteristic of emitter material.

4

Above $\nu_0$: $K_{max}$ depends only on frequency, not intensity. $K_{max}$ increases linearly with $\nu$.

5

Instantaneous emission: time lag < $10^{-9}$ s even for dim light.

11.2

Particle Nature of Light: The Photon

›
Rest Mass
Zero ($m_0=0$)
Speed
$c = 3\times10^8$ m/s always
Energy
$E = h\nu = hc/\lambda$
Momentum
$p = h/\lambda \neq 0$
Charge
Zero — not deflected by E,B
Inertial mass
$m = h\nu/c^2$

Key characteristic

All photons of same frequency $\nu$ have the same energy and momentum, irrespective of intensity. Intensity only affects the number of photons per second.

11.3

Matter Waves — de-Broglie Hypothesis

›

de-Broglie (1924)

Since radiation has dual nature → material particles like electrons should also have wave nature. Wave associated with moving particle = matter wave / de-Broglie wave.

de-Broglie Wavelength

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}$$

Special Cases

Electron accelerated through potential V

$$\lambda_e = \frac{1.227}{\sqrt{V}} \text{ nm}$$

Derived from $KE = eV = p^2/2m \Rightarrow p=\sqrt{2meV}$

Particle with kinetic energy K

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2mK}}$$

Particle at rest ($v=0$)

$\lambda = \infty$ → de-Broglie wave cannot be visualised. Particle must be moving!

Key observations

  • $\lambda \propto 1/v$ — faster particle, shorter wavelength
  • $\lambda \propto 1/m$ — heavier particle, shorter wavelength (same speed)
  • Matter waves are NOT electromagnetic. Travel in vacuum. Not mechanical.
  • Same $\lambda$ → same $p$. Heavier particle moves slower, has less KE.
  • Matter waves are probability waves — amplitude$^2$ ∝ probability of finding particle

Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

$$\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{h}{4\pi}$$

Position and momentum cannot be determined simultaneously with perfect precision — a consequence of wave nature of particles.

⚠️ Blind Spots & Exam Traps

Points where students lose marks. Study these carefully!

🚫

MYTH: More intensity = more KE of photoelectrons

Wrong. Intensity only increases saturation current (more electrons). $K_{max}$ depends only on frequency. Stopping potential is unchanged with intensity.

🚫

MYTH: At threshold frequency, no emission happens

At $\nu = \nu_0$, electrons ARE emitted but with zero kinetic energy. Emission starts exactly at threshold.

🚫

GRAPH TRAP: Slope of $V_0$ vs $\nu$ graph

Slope = $h/e$ (NOT $h$). Y-intercept = $-\phi_0/e$. Slope is the SAME for all materials — only x-intercept ($\nu_0$) differs.

🚫

MYTH: Zero rest mass → zero momentum for photon

Photon momentum $p = h/\lambda \neq 0$. Rest mass = 0 but relativistic momentum is non-zero. This is what drives photoelectric effect.

🚫

FORMULA TRAP: de-Broglie wavelength for proton vs electron

$\lambda_e = 1.227/\sqrt{V}$ nm applies to electrons ONLY. For proton in same V: $\lambda = h/\sqrt{2m_p eV}$ — use the general formula with $m_p$.

🚫

UNIT TRAP: Work function in eV vs Joules

In $K_{max} = h\nu - \phi_0$, use consistent units. Convert $\phi_0$ from eV to J (multiply by $1.6\times10^{-19}$) when $h\nu$ is in Joules.

🚫

TRICK QUESTION: "Electric field of beam becomes zero with frequency f"

The electric field completes two zeros per cycle → actual optical frequency = $f/2$. So photon energy = $h \times (f/2)$.

🚫

COMPARISON: Same λ vs same KE vs same speed

Same $\lambda$ → same $p$ → $v_e > v_p$ (since $m_e < m_p$) and $K_e > K_p$
Same KE → $\lambda_e > \lambda_p$ (since $\lambda = h/\sqrt{2mK}$, smaller $m$ → larger $\lambda$)
Same speed → $\lambda_e > \lambda_p$ (since $\lambda = h/mv$, smaller $m$ → larger $\lambda$)

Ex. 1

Stopping potential → max KE

Potential required to stop ejection of electrons from cathode is 4 V. Find maximum kinetic energy of emitted photoelectrons.

Solution

Using $K_{max} = eV_0$:

$$K_{max} = eV_0 = e \times 4 = 4 \text{ eV}$$

∴ Maximum KE = 4 eV

Ex. 2

Electric field zeros trick — max KE

Electric field of monochromatic beam becomes zero with frequency $2.4\times10^{15}$ Hz. $\phi_0 = 2.0$ eV. Find $KE_{max}$.

Solution

⚠️ E-field becomes zero twice per vibration. So actual optical frequency = $\frac{1}{2}\times 2.4\times10^{15} = 1.2\times10^{15}$ Hz

$$KE_{max} = h\nu - \phi_0 = \frac{6.63\times10^{-34}\times1.2\times10^{15}}{1.6\times10^{-19}} - 2.0 \approx 2.97 \text{ eV}$$
Ex. 3

Caesium — threshold frequency & wavelength

$\phi_0(\text{Cs}) = 2.14$ eV. Find (i) threshold frequency, (ii) wavelength if $V_0 = 0.60$ V.

Solution

(i) Threshold frequency:

$$\nu_0 = \frac{\phi_0}{h} = \frac{2.14\times1.6\times10^{-19}}{6.63\times10^{-34}} \approx 5.16\times10^{14} \text{ Hz}$$

(ii) Wavelength when $V_0 = 0.60$ V:

$$\lambda = \frac{hc}{eV_0 + \phi_0} = \frac{6.63\times10^{-34}\times3\times10^8}{(0.60+2.14)\times1.6\times10^{-19}} \approx 454 \text{ nm}$$

454 nm = blue/violet visible light ✓

Ex. 4

Proton & electron — same de-Broglie wavelength

Proton and electron have same $\lambda$. Which moves faster? Which has more KE?

Solution

Same $\lambda$ → same $p$ (momentum): $p_p = p_e$

$$v = \frac{p}{m} \Rightarrow v_e = \frac{p}{m_e} \gg v_p = \frac{p}{m_p} \quad (m_e \ll m_p)$$

∴ Electron moves faster

$$K = \frac{p^2}{2m} \Rightarrow K_e = \frac{p^2}{2m_e} > K_p = \frac{p^2}{2m_p}$$

∴ Electron has more kinetic energy

Ex. 5

He-Ne Laser — photon count per second

He-Ne laser: $\lambda = 632.8$ nm, power = 9.42 mW. Find (i) energy per photon, (ii) photons/sec.

$$E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = \frac{6.63\times10^{-34}\times3\times10^8}{632.8\times10^{-9}} \approx 3.14\times10^{-19} \text{ J}$$
$$n = \frac{P}{E} = \frac{9.42\times10^{-3}}{3.14\times10^{-19}} \approx 3\times10^{16} \text{ photons/s}$$
Ex. 6

Electron & photon — same wavelength 1 nm

Electron and photon each have $\lambda = 1.00$ nm. Find (i) momenta, (ii) energy of photon, (iii) KE of electron.

Solution

(i) Momenta are equal (same λ):

$$p = \frac{h}{\lambda} = \frac{6.63\times10^{-34}}{1\times10^{-9}} = 6.63\times10^{-25} \text{ kg·m/s}$$

(ii) Photon energy:

$$E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = \frac{6.63\times10^{-34}\times3\times10^8}{1\times10^{-9}} \approx 1.99\times10^{-16} \text{ J}$$

(iii) Electron KE:

$$K = \frac{p^2}{2m_e} = \frac{(6.63\times10^{-25})^2}{2\times9.1\times10^{-31}} \approx 2.41\times10^{-19} \text{ J}$$

Note: Photon energy ≫ electron KE at same wavelength!
