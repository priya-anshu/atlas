Step 01 — Foundation

The Lorentz Magnetic Force

When a charged particle with charge q moves with velocity v inside a region of uniform magnetic field B, it experiences a force called the Lorentz Magnetic Force.

Lorentz Magnetic Force
F = q(v × B) = qvB sin θ
where θ = angle between velocity vector v and magnetic field vector B
q = charge of particle  |  v = speed  |  B = magnetic field strength (Tesla)

Key insight: Since F is always perpendicular to v, the magnetic force does no work on the particle. The speed (magnitude of v) never changes — only the direction changes. Therefore, kinetic energy remains constant.

Step 02 — Critical Concept

Magnetic Field Direction — No More Confusion!

Students often confuse magnetic field direction with north/south poles and field line symbols. Let's fix that once and for all.

🧲 Field Lines Outside a Bar Magnet

Outside a bar magnet, field lines travel from North (N) to South (S) pole. Inside the magnet, they go from S to N.

🤚 Right Hand Rule — Finding Force Direction

To find the direction of F = q(v × B), use the Right Hand Thumb Rule (for positive charge):

👉
Step 1: Point fingers
Point your right-hand fingers in the direction of v (velocity)
🔄
Step 2: Curl toward B
Curl your fingers toward B (magnetic field) direction
👍
Step 3: Thumb = Force
Your thumb points in the direction of F (force) on positive charge
🔄
Negative charge?
Simply reverse the direction of the force found by RHR for negative charges (electrons)
Memory trick: v × B — think of it as "velocity CROSS into B-field". The cross product always gives a vector perpendicular to the plane containing v and B.

Step 03 — Motion Types

How the Particle Moves: 3 Scenarios

⭕
Circular Motion
Condition: v ⊥ B (θ = 90°)
Particle moves in a perfect circle. Force is always perpendicular to velocity — acts as centripetal force.
🌀
Helical Motion
Condition: v at angle θ to B
Velocity has both ⊥ and ∥ components to B. ⊥ component causes circular motion, ∥ component causes forward drift → helix.
➡️
No Deflection
Condition: v ∥ B (θ = 0° or 180°)
No magnetic force acts (F = qvB × 0 = 0). Particle continues in a straight line unaffected.

Step 04 — Full Derivation

Deriving Radius, Period & Frequency

1
The magnetic force provides centripetal force

When a positive charge q moves with speed v perpendicular to field B, the magnetic force F = qvB acts as centripetal force pulling the particle toward center of the circle.

Equating centripetal force and magnetic force
mv²/r = qvB
2
Solving for Radius r

Cancel one v from both sides:

Radius of Circular Path
r = mv / qB
r is proportional to momentum (mv) and inversely proportional to qB

Also in terms of kinetic energy K = ½mv² → p = mv = √(2mK):

Radius in terms of Kinetic Energy
r = √(2mK) / qB
3
Time Period T (time for one full circle)

Circumference of circle = 2πr. Time = distance / speed:

Time Period
T = 2πr/v = 2πm / qB
⭐ Note: T is independent of velocity v and radius r — it only depends on mass, charge and B!
4
Frequency and Angular Frequency
Cyclotron Frequency
f = 1/T = qB / 2πm
Angular Frequency (ω)
ω = 2πf = qB / m
This is called the cyclotron frequency — principle used in particle accelerators!
❓ Why doesn't a faster particle take longer to complete one revolution? ▼
Because a faster particle also has a larger radius (r = mv/qB, so r ∝ v). More circumference to cover, but covers it faster — they exactly cancel out! T = 2πm/qB, which has NO v in it. This is the key principle behind the cyclotron particle accelerator.
❓ What happens if both positive and negative charges enter the same field? ▼
They curve in opposite directions! For a positive charge moving right in a field directed into the page (⊗): force is upward. For a negative charge (same v, same B): force is downward. This is the basis for mass spectrometers and particle detectors.
❓ Does magnetic force change the kinetic energy of the particle? ▼
No! Since F is always perpendicular to v, the work done W = F·d = Fd cos90° = 0. No work → no change in kinetic energy → speed remains constant. Only the direction of velocity changes, not its magnitude. This is fundamentally different from electric force which can change speed.
Step 05 — Advanced Motion

Helical Motion in Magnetic Field

When the velocity v makes an angle θ with the magnetic field B (not 0° and not 90°), the particle follows a helix (spiral path).

Decomposing Velocity

Split v into two components:

Velocity Components
v⊥ = v sin θ   |   v∥ = v cos θ
v⊥ (perpendicular to B) → causes circular motion
v∥ (parallel to B) → no force, particle drifts forward at constant speed

Helical Parameters

ParameterFormulaWhat it means
Radius of helixr = mv⊥ / qB = mv sinθ / qBRadius of the circular part
Time PeriodT = 2πm / qBSame as circular motion!
Pitch (P)P = v∥ × T = v cosθ × 2πm/qBDistance moved along B in one full revolution
Pitch = the linear distance traveled along the axis (field direction) in one complete circular revolution. Like the "step" of a screw thread.

⚠️ Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Thinking T depends on speed ▼
T = 2πm/qB — there is no v! Time period is completely independent of speed. This surprises most students. A fast proton and a slow proton in the same field complete one revolution in the same time.
Mistake 2: Confusing field direction (⊗ vs ⊙) ▼
⊗ means into the page (like looking at the tail feathers of an arrow flying away from you). ⊙ means out of the page (like the tip of an arrow coming toward you). Always verify this before applying RHR.
Mistake 3: Forgetting to split velocity for helical motion ▼
When velocity is at angle θ to B, always resolve: v⊥ = v sinθ and v∥ = v cosθ. Use v⊥ for radius and period, v∥ for pitch. Never use total v for radius in helical motion.
Mistake 4: Applying RHR for negative charges without reversing ▼
RHR gives direction for positive charges. For negative charges (electrons), the force is exactly opposite. Always flip the direction for −ve charges. An electron moving right in ⊗ field curves downward, not upward.
