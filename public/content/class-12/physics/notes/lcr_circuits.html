Building Block 1

AC Circuit Containing Resistance and Capacitance

R and C are connected in series to an AC source E. Current through both is the same (series circuit), but voltage across each lags/leads differently.

1

Voltage across R is in phase with current; voltage across C lags current by 90°

V_R = I₀R (in phase with I)  |  V_C = I₀X_C (lags I by π/2)

2

Add as phasors (NOT algebraically) — V_R and V_C are 90° apart

Resultant peak EMF

E₀ = √[(I₀R)² + (I₀X_C)²] = I₀√(R² + X_C²)

3

Define Impedance Z = E₀/I₀

Impedance of R-C circuit

Z = √(R² + X_C²)

Phase angle: tan φ = X_C/R = (1/ωC)/R. In an R-C circuit, voltage lags behind current by phase angle φ (opposite of what happens in pure inductor where voltage leads).

Static Reference — Phasor Diagram (R-C)

V_R along current axis (horizontal); V_C drawn 90° below (lagging); E₀ is the vector sum (hypotenuse).

Combining Everything

AC Through L-C-R Circuit

R, L, C connected in series to an AC source. Current through all three is identical in amplitude and phase: I = I₀sinωt. But each element's voltage has a different phase relationship with this current.

ElementPeak VoltagePhase vs Current
Resistor RV_R = I₀RIn phase
Inductor LV_L = I₀X_LLeads I by 90°
Capacitor CV_C = I₀X_CLags I by 90°
1

V_L and V_C are exactly opposite in direction (180° apart from each other)

Since V_L leads I by 90° and V_C lags I by 90°, they point in opposite directions on the phasor diagram. Net vertical voltage = V_L − V_C (assuming V_L > V_C).

2

Combine (V_L − V_C) with V_R using Pythagoras (they're 90° apart)

Resultant peak EMF

E₀ = √[(I₀R)² + (I₀X_L − I₀X_C)²] = I₀√[R² + (X_L−X_C)²]

3

Impedance and phase angle of LCR circuit

Impedance

Z = E₀/I₀ = √[R² + (X_L−X_C)²]

Phase angle

tan φ = (X_L − X_C)/R

The reactance X = X_L − X_C is the net "opposing" component, separate from R. The right-angled triangle with R, X (=X_L−X_C), and Z as hypotenuse is called the impedance diagram — memorize this triangle, it solves 90% of LCR numerical problems instantly.

The complete alternating current equation: I = I₀ sin(ωt + φ') where φ' may be positive or negative depending on whether X_L > X_C or X_C > X_L (explained fully in Special Cases below).

Static Reference — Phasor Diagram (Series L-C-R)

V_L (up) and V_C (down) are opposite; their difference combines with V_R (horizontal) at 90° to give resultant E₀.

Static Reference — Impedance Diagram (Right Triangle)

Base = R, Perpendicular = X (= X_L − X_C), Hypotenuse = Z. This triangle directly gives tan φ = X/R and Z = √(R²+X²).

Worked Example

Impedance — Full Numerical Walkthrough

Example 7 (from book): C=100μF, R=50Ω, L=0.5H, connected in series to 110V-50Hz source. Find RMS current.

X_C = 1/(2πνC) = 1/(2×3.14×50×100×10⁻⁶) = 31.85 Ω

X_L = ωL = 2πνL = 2×3.14×50×0.5 = 157 Ω

Z = √[R² + (X_L−X_C)²] = √[(50)² + (157−31.85)²] = √[2500+15660.5] = 134.77 Ω

E_rms = 110V (given as RMS already) → I_rms = E_rms/Z = 110/134.77 = 0.816 A

Note the trap: Since X_L (157Ω) > X_C (31.85Ω), the circuit is inductance-dominated — voltage leads current. If the question had asked for phase angle direction, you'd say "voltage leads current by φ = tan⁻¹[(157−31.85)/50] ≈ 68.2°".

Example 8: L=0.01H, R=1Ω coil connected to 200V, 50Hz AC. Find impedance and time lag.

Z = √(R² + X_L²) = √[1² + (2π×50×0.01)²] = √(1+9.86) = √10.86 = 3.3 Ω

tan φ = ωL/R = (2×3.14×50×0.01)/1 = 3.14 → φ = tan⁻¹(3.14) ≈ 72°

Time lag Δt = φ/ω = (72π/180)/(2π×50) = 1/250 s = 4 ms

Why convert phase angle to time? φ is in radians (angle), but "time lag" must be in seconds. Use Δt = φ/ω — this conversion is asked frequently and many students forget the ω in the denominator.

The Most Tested Concept

Resonance in Series LCR Circuit

Definition (exact): When the phase difference φ between current and voltage becomes zero, the circuit is said to be in resonance. This happens when X_L = X_C exactly.
1

As frequency increases, X_L increases and X_C decreases

X_L = ωL (∝ω, increasing)  |  X_C = 1/ωC (∝1/ω, decreasing). At some specific angular frequency ω_r, they become exactly equal.

2

Set X_L = X_C and solve for ω_r

ω_rL = 1/(ω_rC)  ⟹  ω_r² = 1/(LC)

3

Resonant Angular Frequency

ω_r = 1/√(LC)

Resonant Frequency (ν_r)

ν_r = 1/(2π√(LC))

At resonance — three things happen simultaneously (memorize all three):
  1. Impedance is minimum: Z = R (since X_L−X_C=0, only R remains)
  2. Current is maximum: I₀ = E₀/Z = E₀/R
  3. The circuit behaves as a purely resistive circuit (φ=0, current and voltage in phase)

Real-world application: This exact principle is used in the tuning mechanism of a radio or TV — turning the tuning knob changes C (or L), shifting ω_r to match the broadcast frequency you want to pick up. Only that frequency produces maximum current/signal strength; all others are suppressed.

Static Reference — Resonance Curve (I₀ vs ω for R₁ > R₂ > R₃)

Peak occurs at the same ω_r regardless of R — but lower resistance gives a sharper, taller peak (higher selectivity). This sharpness is called the "quality factor" or Q of the circuit.

Energy Dissipation

Average Power in AC Circuit

In AC, both EMF and current change continuously with time — so we must average power over a complete cycle (0 to T), not just compute it at one instant.

1

Instantaneous power and instantaneous work

P = EI = E₀sinωt · I₀sin(ωt+φ)   |   dW = EI dt

2

Integrate over one full cycle (0 to T) — expand using trig identity

Using sin(ωt+φ) = sinωt cosφ + cosωt sinφ, then integrating: ∫sin²ωt dt over one period gives T/2, while ∫sinωt cosωt dt = 0. The sinφ term vanishes completely!

3

Result: Total work, then average power

W = (E₀I₀T/2) cosφ

Average Power — Master Formula

P_av = (E₀I₀/2) cosφ = E_rms · I_rms · cosφ

Alternate form

P_av = I²Z cosφ

Power Factor

cosφ = P_av/(E_rms·I_rms) = R/Z

Power factor cosφ = R/Z is dimensionless, ranges 0 to 1, and tells you what FRACTION of the apparent power (E_rms×I_rms) is actually converted to useful work (true power). cosφ = "True power / Apparent power".

Power in Each Pure Element (memorize all three results)

ElementφAverage Power
Pure R0°P_av = E_v·I_v (MAXIMUM)
Pure L90°P_av = 0 (ZERO)
Pure C90°P_av = 0 (ZERO)
Why is power zero in pure L and pure C? Because cosφ = cos90° = 0. Physically: energy is stored in the magnetic field (L) or electric field (C) during one quarter cycle and given back to the source during the next quarter cycle — net energy consumed over a full cycle is exactly zero. This is called wattless current or idle current.

Power Factor — All Circuit Combinations

(i) Pure R: φ=0 → P_av = E_vI_v (maximum possible)
(ii) Pure L: φ=π/2 → P_av = 0
(iii) Pure C: φ=π/2 → P_av = 0
(iv) L and R: tanφ=ωL/R → cosφ=R/√(R²+ω²L²) → P_av = E_vI_v·R/√(R²+ω²L²)
(v) C and R: tanφ=1/(ωCR) → cosφ=R/√(R²+1/ω²C²) → P_av = E_vI_v·R/√(R²+1/ω²C²)
(vi) L, C and R: tanφ=(ωL−1/ωC)/R → cosφ=R/√[R²+(ωL−1/ωC)²] → P_av = E_vI_v·R/Z

Example 10: E₀=283V, ν=50Hz, R=3Ω, L=25.48mH, C=796μF — find impedance, phase, power, power factor.

X_L = 2πνL = 2×3.14×50×25.48×10⁻³ ≈ 8Ω

X_C = 1/(2πνC) = 1/(2×3.14×50×796×10⁻⁶) ≈ 4Ω

Z = √[R²+(X_L−X_C)²] = √[3²+(8−4)²] = √(9+16) = √25 = 5Ω

φ = tan⁻¹[(X_L−X_C)/R] = tan⁻¹(4/3) = 53.1° (current lags voltage since X_L>X_C)

cosφ = R/Z = 3/5 = 0.6

Critical for Exams

Special Cases — Comparing X_L and X_C

⚖️

X_L = X_C

Z = R, tanφ = 0, φ = 0°. Circuit is purely resistive — this is the resonance condition.

⬆️

X_L > X_C

tanφ is positive. Voltage leads current — circuit is inductance-dominated (inductive circuit).

⬇️

X_C > X_L

tanφ is negative. Voltage lags current (current leads) — circuit is capacitance-dominated (capacitive circuit).

Where Marks Get Lost

Blind Spots — Read Before Every Exam

Blind spot 1: Adding V_R, V_L, V_C algebraically instead of as phasors ▾
This is the #1 mistake. V_R, V_L, V_C peak at different times (different phases), so you can NEVER write E₀ = V_R + V_L + V_C directly. You must use E₀ = √[V_R² + (V_L−V_C)²]. The only exception: if asked for INSTANTANEOUS values at one specific frozen moment with known phase angles, you can substitute numbers — but the general peak-value relationship is always the vector sum.
Blind spot 2: Forgetting which reactance term goes first in (X_L − X_C) ▾
Always write X = X_L − X_C (inductive minus capacitive), never the reverse. The SIGN of this result tells you the circuit's nature: positive → inductive (V leads I), negative → capacitive (I leads V). If you accidentally flip the subtraction order, you'll get the phase direction backwards even if the magnitude (squared in Z) comes out the same.
Blind spot 3: At resonance, thinking voltage across L and C individually becomes zero too ▾
At resonance, X_L = X_C, so the NET reactive voltage (V_L − V_C) becomes zero — but V_L and V_C individually can still be very large (and equal to each other)! In fact, at resonance with low R, V_L and V_C can both be many times larger than the source voltage E. This is called voltage magnification and is a classic "explain why" question.
Blind spot 4: Confusing power factor (cosφ) with phase angle (φ) itself ▾
Power factor is cosφ (a number between 0 and 1), NOT φ (an angle). If a question asks "find power factor," your final numeric answer should be a pure number like 0.6 or 0.866 — not an angle in degrees. If it asks "find phase angle," THEN you give degrees or radians via tan⁻¹.
Blind spot 5: Phasor diagrams "don't convey initial conditions" — limitation explicitly noted in NCERT ▾
The book explicitly states: phasor diagrams only depict the steady-state solution (relative phase angle), not transient/initial-condition behavior right after switching the circuit on. Don't use phasor diagrams to analyze what happens in the first few milliseconds after a switch closes — they assume the AC has already settled into steady oscillation.
Blind spot 6: Mixing up resonant ANGULAR frequency (ω_r) with resonant frequency (ν_r) ▾
ω_r = 1/√(LC) is in rad/s. ν_r = 1/(2π√(LC)) is in Hz. They differ by a factor of 2π. If a question gives you L and C and asks for "resonant frequency," check carefully whether it wants ω_r or ν_r — plugging into the wrong formula form is a very common silly mistake under exam pressure.
