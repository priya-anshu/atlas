Ampere's Circuital Law

The magnetic equivalent of Gauss's Law

Complete derivation · Physical meaning at every step · Applications to solenoid & toroid · Interactive visualizations

Ampere's Circuital Law
∮ B·dl = μ₀Ienc
Closed line integral of B equals μ₀ times enclosed current
1

Statement of Ampere's Circuital Law

The Law

The line integral of the magnetic field B along any closed path (Amperian loop) equals μ₀ times the net current passing through the surface bounded by that closed path.

Mathematical form
∮ B·dl = μ₀Ienc
∮ = closed line integral
B = magnetic field vector
dl = small element of the loop
μ₀ = 4π×10⁻⁷ T·m/A (permeability)
I_enc = net current enclosed
💡 Analogy with Gauss's Law

Just as Gauss's Law relates electric flux through a closed surface to enclosed charge,
Ampere's Law relates the line integral of B around a closed loop to enclosed current.

Gauss: ∮ E·dA = q_enc/ε₀
Ampere: ∮ B·dl = μ₀·I_enc
Concept diagram
⚠ Important note

The choice of Amperian loop is free — you can pick any shape. But a clever choice that matches the symmetry of the problem makes B·dl constant, allowing you to pull B out of the integral.

2

Physical Meaning — What Does It Say?

Ampere's Law tells us that magnetic field lines always form closed loops around currents. The strength of this "circulation" is proportional to how much current passes through the loop.

B-field encircles I

For any loop enclosing the wire, ∮B·dl = μ₀I regardless of loop shape.

No enclosed I → zero

If the loop encloses no current, ∮B·dl = 0 even though B may exist at the loop.

Multiple currents

I_enc is algebraic sum. Opposite directions subtract. ∮B·dl = μ₀(I₁−I₂).

Why B·dl and not just B?

B·dl = |B||dl|cosθ, the dot product.
We only count the component of B parallel to the path. When B is everywhere tangent to the Amperian circle (as for a long wire), cosθ = 1 everywhere, so B comes out of the integral cleanly:

∮ B·dl = B ∮ dl = B(2πr)
∴ B(2πr) = μ₀I
∴ B = μ₀I/2πr ← Biot-Savart result!
3

Setting Up the Proof — Infinite Straight Wire

We prove Ampere's Law starting from Biot–Savart Law for the simplest case: an infinitely long straight wire carrying current I. We choose an Amperian circle of radius r centred on the wire.

From Biot–Savart (already proved)
B = μ₀I / 2πr
for any point at distance r from infinite wire
Choice of Amperian loop
Why circle?

A circle of radius r centred on the wire exploits the cylindrical symmetry. B has the same magnitude everywhere on this circle and is always tangent to it.

B∥dl

Since B is tangential and dl is also tangential at every point, the angle between them is 0°, so B·dl = B·dl·cos0° = B·dl.

💡 Key symmetry argument

Because the wire is infinite and straight, the field is the same everywhere at radius r. So B is constant along the Amperian circle. This lets us pull B outside the integral!

3D view — wire + Amperian circle

Step-by-Step Derivation

Starting from Biot–Savart law for an infinite wire, we derive Ampere's law rigorously. Each step is explained with the reason.

A
Known: field from Biot–Savart
B = μ₀I / 2πr
At every point on the Amperian circle of radius r, B has this magnitude (already proved using Biot-Savart)
B
Write the line integral
∮ B·dl = ∮ B·dl·cosθ

B is tangential to the Amperian circle (given by right-hand rule for wire). dl is also tangential (it's a small arc element along the circle). So the angle θ between B and dl is 0°.

cosθ = cos0° = 1
∴ ∮ B·dl = ∮ B·dl
C
Pull B out of the integral (it's constant!)
∮ B·dl = B ∮ dl

Why can B come out? Due to cylindrical symmetry of the infinite wire, B has the same magnitude at every point on the circle (only r matters). So B is a constant with respect to the integration variable dl. A constant can always be taken outside an integral.

D
Evaluate ∮ dl = circumference = 2πr
∮ dl = 2πr

The closed integral of dl around the full circle is simply the circumference of the circle = 2πr. This is always true for any circular Amperian loop.

E
Substitute B = μ₀I/2πr
∮ B·dl = B × 2πr
= (μ₀I/2πr) × 2πr
✓
Final result — 2πr cancels perfectly!
Ampere's Circuital Law — Derived!
∮ B·dl = μ₀I
The 2πr in numerator and denominator cancel → the result is independent of r!
This is the deep beauty: the loop integral doesn't depend on which circle you choose.

Direction Convention & Sign of I_enc

Right-hand rule for Amperian loop
Step 1

Choose a direction to traverse the Amperian loop (clockwise or anti-clockwise).

Step 2

Curl the fingers of the right hand in the direction of traversal → the thumb points in the positive normal direction of the surface.

Step 3

Current flowing in the direction of positive normal → positive. Current flowing opposite → negative.

General form with sign
∮ B·dl = μ₀(I₁ - I₂ + I₃ …)
I_enc = algebraic sum
Common mistake to avoid

B on the left side is the total field — from ALL sources, not just the enclosed current. But the right side only counts currents passing through the loop surface.

Sign convention examples
💡 Why the result is independent of loop shape?

For any closed path (circle, square, irregular), ∮B·dl = μ₀I_enc. This is because the magnetic field of a current has the property that its circulation depends only on how many current lines pierce the enclosed surface — not the shape of the boundary.

Application 1 — Magnetic Field of a Solenoid

A solenoid is a long helical coil of wire. We use Ampere's law with a rectangular Amperian loop to find B inside.

Assumptions (ideal solenoid)
• Length L ≫ radius → field uniform inside
• n turns per unit length
• B is parallel to axis (by symmetry)
• B = 0 outside (for ideal solenoid)
Side ab
Inside solenoid, B∥dl
Contribution = B·L
Side cd
Outside solenoid, B = 0
Contribution = 0
Sides bc, da
B ⊥ dl (perpendicular)
Contribution = 0 (cos90°=0)
Applying Ampere's Law
∮ B·dl = B·L + 0 + 0 + 0 = BL
I_enc = n·L·I (n turns in length L)
∴ BL = μ₀·n·L·I
Field inside solenoid
B = μ₀nI
n = turns per unit length, L cancels

Application 2 — Magnetic Field of a Toroid

A toroid is a solenoid bent into a donut (torus) shape. The field is confined inside the toroid core.

Three Amperian loops to consider
Loop 1: r < r₁ (inside hole)
I_enc = 0 (no current pierces)
∴ B = 0 inside the hole
Loop 2: r₁ < r < r₂ (inside core)
I_enc = N·I (N turns enclosed)
B·2πr = μ₀NI
∴ B = μ₀NI / 2πr
Loop 3: r > r₂ (outside toroid)
I_enc = NI − NI = 0 (net current = 0)
∴ B = 0 outside
💡 Why B=0 outside toroid?

For a loop outside the toroid, the current goes up through one side of each turn and down through the other. Net enclosed current = NI − NI = 0. So B = 0 outside. This is why toroids are used in transformers — no external field leakage!
