Section 8.1 — The Missing Piece

Displacement Current

Ampere's circuital law (the OLD version, before Maxwell) states that the line integral of magnetic field B around any closed path equals μ₀ times the total current threading that closed path:

Ampere's Circuital Law (original, incomplete)

∮B·dl = μ₀I

The problem Maxwell spotted: When a capacitor is charging, current flows through the connecting wires (conduction current), but no actual charge flows through the gap between the plates — yet a changing electric field exists there. Ampere's original law gives inconsistent answers depending on which surface you choose to apply it to near a charging capacitor.
1

Maxwell's fix: a changing electric field is equivalent to a current

As the capacitor charges, charge accumulates on the plates, producing a changing electric field between them. Maxwell proposed this changing E field acts exactly like a current — he called it displacement current (I_d).

2

Electric field between capacitor plates

E = σ/ε₀ = q/(ε₀A)   [σ = charge density, A = plate area]

3

Rate of change of this field

dE/dt = (1/ε₀A)·(dq/dt) = I/(ε₀A)   [since I = dq/dt]

4

Define displacement current using flux of E (Φ_E = EA)

Displacement Current — Master Formula

I_d = ε₀(dΦ_E/dt) = ε₀A(dE/dt)

Remarkably, I_d works out to be numerically EQUAL to the conduction current I flowing through the wires — they're two faces of the same underlying current.

Static Reference — Conduction Current (wires) vs Displacement Current (gap)

I_c flows through the connecting wires; I_d "flows" conceptually through the gap between plates due to changing E field. I_c = I_d at every instant during charging.

Example 1: Voltage across 2.0μF capacitor changing at 10 V/s — find displacement current.

I_d = ε₀(dΦ_E/dt) = ε₀·d(EA)/dt = ε₀A·d(V/d)/dt = (ε₀A/d)·dV/dt = C·dV/dt

I_d = C × dV/dt = 2.0×10⁻⁶ × 10 = 20 μA

Shortcut worth memorizing: I_d = C(dV/dt) — this is often faster than going through E and A separately, especially when C is given directly.

The Complete Picture

Maxwell's Equations

1

Total current = conduction current + displacement current

I = I_c + I_d

2

Modified Ampere's Circuital Law (Ampere-Maxwell Law)

Final corrected form

∮B·dl = μ₀(I_c + I_d) = μ₀[I_c + ε₀(dΦ_E/dt)]

All Four Maxwell's Equations (Vacuum)

Gauss's Law (Electrostatics)

∮E·dS = q/ε₀

Electric flux through closed surface ∝ enclosed charge

Gauss's Law (Magnetism)

∮B·dS = 0

Magnetic flux through ANY closed surface is always zero — no magnetic monopoles exist

Faraday's Law of EM Induction

∮E·dl = −dΦ_B/dt

A changing B field induces a circulating E field

Ampere-Maxwell Circuital Law

∮B·dl = μ₀[I_c + ε₀(dΦ_E/dt)]

A changing E field (or conduction current) induces a circulating B field

The beautiful symmetry: Faraday's law says changing B creates E. The Ampere-Maxwell law (with displacement current added) says changing E creates B. This mutual generation is EXACTLY what allows an electromagnetic wave to self-propagate through empty space — neither field needs an external source once the wave exists; each field's change recreates the other.

Section 8.2

Source of Electromagnetic Waves

The Source Condition

Only an ACCELERATING (or decelerating) charge produces EM waves

A charge moving with constant velocity does NOT radiate EM waves — only non-zero acceleration produces changing E and B fields that propagate as a self-sustaining wave.

Frequency relation

Frequency of EM wave = Frequency of oscillation of the charge producing it

X-rays — a specific example: When fast-moving electrons are suddenly stopped by a metal target of high atomic number, the abrupt deceleration produces electromagnetic waves called X-rays. This is a textbook example of "decelerated charge → EM radiation."

Geometry of the Wave

Transverse Nature of Electromagnetic Waves

EM waves are transverse — meaning the oscillating quantities are perpendicular to the direction of travel, not along it (unlike sound waves, which are longitudinal).

Three Mutually Perpendicular Directions

E ⊥ B    B ⊥ (direction of propagation)    E ⊥ (direction of propagation)

This is why EM waves can be polarised — only transverse waves support polarization, since you can restrict the oscillation direction within the plane perpendicular to propagation.

Static Reference — Plane EM Wave Travelling Along Z-axis

E oscillates in the X-Z plane (vertical here), B oscillates in the Y-Z plane (horizontal here) — both perpendicular to each other and to the Z-axis direction of travel.

Mathematical Form

Equations of E and B for a Travelling Wave

For an EM wave travelling in the +Z direction, with E along X-axis and B along Y-axis:

E = E_x = E₀ sin(kz − ωt)     B = B_y = B₀ sin(kz − ωt)

k (wave number)k = 2π/λ
ω (angular frequency)ω = 2πν
E₀, B₀amplitudes of varying electric/magnetic field

Relation between amplitudes

E₀/B₀ = c

This holds for instantaneous values too: E/B = c at every point and every instant — not just for the peaks.

Speed of EM Wave

In free space (vacuum)

c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀) = 3×10⁸ m/s

In a material medium

v = 1/√(με) = c/√(μ_rε_r) = c/n

n = refractive index. Since light is an EM wave, its speed in a medium depends entirely on the medium's electric permittivity and magnetic permeability — this is WHY refraction happens at all.

Example 3: EM wave at 3×10⁸ m/s in vacuum enters medium with ε_r=2, μ_r=1. Find new velocity.

v = c/√(μ_rε_r) = 3×10⁸/√(1×2) = 3×10⁸/√2 = (3/√2)×10⁸ m/s ≈ 2.12×10⁸ m/s

All 10 Properties — Exact Book List

Important Characteristics of EM Waves

  1. Produced by accelerated charge — constant velocity charges don't radiate.
  2. Don't require any material medium for propagation — can travel through vacuum (unlike sound).
  3. Travel in free space with speed of light, c = 3×10⁸ m/s, given by c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀).
  4. Speed in a medium depends on the material's electric and magnetic properties: v = 1/√(με) = c/n.
  5. E and B variations are perpendicular to each other AND to the direction of propagation.
  6. E and B are in the same phase in free space — they reach maximum and zero together, related by E₀/B₀ = c.
  7. Follow the superposition principle — show reflection, refraction, interference, diffraction, polarisation, exactly like light waves (because light IS an EM wave).
  8. Average electric and magnetic energy densities are equal: (½ε₀E²)_av = (B²/2μ₀)_av.
  9. Electric vector is the "light vector" — E is responsible for all optical effects we see; this is why it's specifically called the light vector.
  10. EM waves carry both energy and momentum: E = hc/λ (energy of photon form), p = U/c = mc (momentum).

Static Reference — E and B Oscillating in Phase

E and B reach their peaks and zeros at exactly the same z (or t) — this in-phase relationship is what makes energy density equal on average for both fields.

Where Marks Get Lost

Blind Spots — Read Before Every Exam

Blind spot 1: Thinking displacement current is "fake" or just a mathematical trick ▾
Displacement current produces a REAL magnetic field, exactly like conduction current does. It is not a "fix" applied only on paper — it's a genuine physical source of B field. The only difference from conduction current is that no actual charge crosses the capacitor gap; it's the CHANGING electric field itself that generates B, via the Ampere-Maxwell law.
Blind spot 2: Confusing I_d (displacement current) with actual charge flow ▾
I_d = ε₀(dΦ_E/dt) has units of current (Amperes) and behaves like current for magnetic field purposes, but NO charge physically moves through the gap between capacitor plates. It exists purely because the electric field there is changing with time. If the field were constant (steady DC, fully charged capacitor), I_d = 0 even though E ≠ 0.
Blind spot 3: Assuming a uniformly moving charge also produces EM waves ▾
A charge moving with CONSTANT velocity has a static (though moving) field pattern in its own reference frame — it does NOT radiate. Only ACCELERATION (or deceleration) — a change in velocity — produces genuine EM radiation. This is why antennas use oscillating (accelerating) charges, and why X-rays come from electrons that are suddenly decelerated (not from electrons moving steadily).
Blind spot 4: Forgetting EM waves need NO medium — common trap question ▾
Unlike sound (mechanical wave, needs medium) and unlike water waves, EM waves travel through a perfect vacuum just fine — that's how sunlight reaches Earth through empty space. A "medium" only SLOWS DOWN an EM wave (v = c/n) — it is never required for the wave to exist or propagate at all.
Blind spot 5: Mixing up which field (E or B) is responsible for optical/light effects ▾
It is specifically the electric field vector E that is called the "light vector" and is responsible for optical effects (what your eyes/detectors respond to). B exists and carries equal average energy, but E is the one tied to vision and most optical phenomena measurement.
Blind spot 6: Using k and ω without checking they match the SAME wave equation argument ▾
In E = E₀sin(kz−ωt), both k and ω must appear with the SAME sign pattern relative to z and t for the wave to travel in the +Z direction. If you see (kz+ωt) instead, that represents a wave travelling in the −Z direction. Always check the sign before assuming direction of propagation in a numerical problem.
Blind spot 7: Thinking refractive index only depends on ε_r, ignoring μ_r ▾
n = √(μ_rε_r), not just √ε_r. For most ordinary transparent materials, μ_r ≈ 1 so this distinction doesn't matter numerically — but conceptually and in exam derivations, you must keep μ_r in the formula. CBSE numericals (like Example 3) specifically test whether you include μ_r or drop it by mistake.
