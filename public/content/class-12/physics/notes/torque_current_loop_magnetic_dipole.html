Consider a rectangular current loop ABCD suspended in a uniform magnetic field B. This is the core geometry for understanding how motors, galvanometers, and all rotating electrical machines work.

Step by Step

Force on Each Arm of the Loop

The key formula is F = BIl sinθ. Apply this to each of the 4 arms separately. The angle θ here is between the current direction in that arm and the field B.

1
Arms AD and BC (length = b, parallel to rotation axis)

Current in AD is parallel (or anti-parallel) to B. So θ = 0° or 180°.

Force on AD and BC
F = BIb sin0° = 0
These two arms produce zero force — they contribute nothing to torque. Ignore them.
2
Arms AB and CD (length = a, perpendicular to rotation axis)

Current in AB and CD is always perpendicular to B (θ = 90°). These are the arms that experience force.

Force on arm AB
F₁ = BIa sin90° = BIa
Force on arm CD
F₂ = BIa sin90° = BIa

The magnitudes are equal: F₁ = F₂ = BIa

Critical: F₁ and F₂ are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction! So net force = F₁ − F₂ = 0. The loop doesn't translate (move linearly). But since they act on different arms (separated by distance b), they form a couple that produces TORQUE.
3
Why does a "couple" produce torque even with zero net force?

Two equal, opposite, parallel forces separated by a perpendicular distance form a couple. A couple has zero net force but non-zero torque: τ = F × d (perpendicular distance between the forces).

Special Case

Case I — Magnetic Field B is in the Plane of the Loop

📐
Condition
The plane of loop ABCD lies in the same plane as the field B. The area vector (normal to loop) is perpendicular to B.
🔄
What happens?
F₁ acts upward on AB, F₂ acts downward on CD. They form a couple. The perpendicular distance between them = full breadth b.

Torque Derivation for Case I

1
Forces form a couple

F₁ (on AB) = BIa upward  |  F₂ (on CD) = BIa downward

They don't cancel rotationally because they act on opposite arms.

2
Torque = Force × Perpendicular distance
Torque due to couple (Case I, θ = 90°)
τ = F₁ × b = BIa × b = BIab = BIA
Since A = ab (area of loop), τ = BIA. This is the maximum torque — happens when B is in the plane of the loop.
3
Direction of rotation

Using the right hand rule: the loop rotates in the anti-clockwise direction (when viewed from the front, with B pointing right and current flowing as shown in NCERT diagram).

Maximum Torque (Case I — B in plane of loop)
τ_max = BIA
This is the maximum possible torque. The loop will rotate away from this position — this is an unstable equilibrium position in the context of a motor.

General Case

Case II — Plane of Loop at Angle θ to Magnetic Field

📏
Condition
The plane of the loop makes an angle φ with B. Equivalently, the normal to the loop (area vector) makes angle θ = (90° − φ) with B.
📐
Key difference
The perpendicular distance between F₁ and F₂ is no longer the full breadth b. It reduces to b sinθ where θ is angle between area normal and B.

Step-by-Step Derivation

1
Forces on AB and CD — still F = BIa each

Current in AB and CD is always perpendicular to B (the loop can only rotate about its central axis, keeping AB and CD perpendicular to B). So F₁ = F₂ = BIa. This doesn't change with angle.

2
Effective perpendicular distance reduces

When the plane makes angle φ with B, the perpendicular distance between the lines of action of F₁ and F₂ is not b — it is b sinθ (where θ is angle between area normal and B field).

Geometry trick: If the loop plane makes angle φ with B, then the area normal makes (90° − φ) with B. Call this angle θ. The perpendicular arm of the couple = b sinθ.
3
Torque = F × perpendicular arm
Torque for General Case II
τ = F₁ × (b sinθ) = BIa × b sinθ = BIAb sinθ
Where A = ab is area of loop and θ is the angle between area normal (m̂) and field B.
4
For N turns of the coil
Final Torque Formula (N-turn coil)
τ = NIAB sinθ
N = number of turns, I = current, A = area, B = field strength, θ = angle between area normal and B

Full Torque Derivation — Both Cases Together

Compact Step-by-Step

1
Loop ABCD in field B. AB = CD = a (active arms). AD = BC = b.

Current direction: A→B→C→D→A (counterclockwise when viewed from front).

2
Force on AD = 0 and BC = 0

These run parallel/anti-parallel to B → sin0° = 0 → F = 0.

3
Force on AB: F₁ = BIa (say, upward ↑)
Force on CD: F₂ = BIa (downward ↓)

Equal and opposite → net translational force = 0, but they create a couple!

4
Torque by each force about the central axis (MM')
Torque from F₁ (on AB)
τ₁ = F₁ × (a/2) sinθ = BIa × (a/2) sinθ
Torque from F₂ (on CD) — same direction!
τ₂ = F₂ × (a/2) sinθ = BIa × (a/2) sinθ
5
Total torque τ = τ₁ + τ₂
Net Torque on loop (single turn)
τ = BIa × (a/2) sinθ + BIa × (a/2) sinθ = BIa² sinθ
Wait — this uses a² because the torque arm is a/2 from MM' for each force, where MM' bisects AB and CD. But the full derivation using couple gives: τ = BIab sinθ = BIA sinθ, since A = ab for rectangular loop.
✅ Correct Final Result (using couple approach)
τ = BIab sinθ = BIA sinθ
Couple torque = one force × perpendicular distance = BIa × b sinθ = BIAb sinθ... with A = ab gives τ = BIA sinθ ✓
6
For a coil of N turns
Master Formula — Torque on N-turn coil
τ = NIAB sinθ
N = number of turns  |  I = current (A)  |  A = area (m²)  |  B = field (T)
θ = angle between area normal m̂ and field B
❓ Why do F₁ and F₂ add up in torque instead of cancelling? ▼
F₁ and F₂ are equal and opposite, so they cancel for linear (translational) motion. But torque depends on force × perpendicular arm about a specific axis. Both F₁ (upward on AB) and F₂ (downward on CD) try to rotate the loop in the same direction about the central axis MM'. So their torques ADD, not cancel. This is the definition of a couple.
❓ Why is θ the angle between area normal and B, not between loop plane and B? ▼
The area vector (m̂) is defined as perpendicular to the plane of the loop. So if the loop plane makes angle φ with B, the area normal makes angle (90°−φ) with B. We call this (90°−φ) as θ. The torque formula τ = BIA sinθ uses θ as angle between normal to loop and B. At θ=90°, normal ⊥ B means B is in the plane → maximum torque. At θ=0°, normal ∥ B → zero torque (equilibrium).
Magnetic Dipole Moment

The Loop as a Magnetic Dipole (m)

The torque formula τ = NIAB sinθ looks exactly like the torque on an electric dipole in an electric field: τ = pE sinθ. This analogy is not a coincidence — a current loop IS a magnetic dipole.

Magnetic Dipole Moment
m = NIA
m = magnetic dipole moment (A·m²)  |  N = turns  |  I = current  |  A = area
Direction of m: given by right hand rule — curl fingers in direction of current → thumb points along m (area normal).

Torque in Vector Form

Vector form of torque (elegant!)
τ = m × B
|τ| = mB sinθ = NIAB sinθ ✓
This is the most compact and powerful form — it tells you both magnitude AND direction of torque via cross product.

Equilibrium positions: When θ=0° (m ∥ B), τ=0 — this is stable equilibrium (like a compass needle aligned with Earth's field). When θ=180° (m anti-parallel to B), τ=0 again — but this is unstable equilibrium (any small disturbance flips it).
❓ A bar magnet is also a magnetic dipole — how does it relate? ▼
A bar magnet has a north and south pole separated by distance 2l. Its magnetic dipole moment is m = p_m = M = m·2l (from S to N inside the magnet). A current loop with moment m = NIA behaves identically to a bar magnet of the same dipole moment. This is why solenoids (multi-turn coils) behave exactly like bar magnets — they have N and S poles at the two ends!
❓ What's the unit and direction of magnetic dipole moment? ▼
Unit: A·m² (ampere × metre squared) or equivalently J/T (joule per tesla).
Direction: Use the right-hand thumb rule — curl the fingers of your right hand in the direction of conventional current flow in the loop. Your thumb points in the direction of the magnetic moment m (which is the same as the area vector direction, i.e., normal to the loop plane following the right-hand rule).

⚠️ Common Exam Mistakes

Mistake 1: Using wrong angle in τ = NIAB sinθ ▼
θ is the angle between the area normal (m̂) and B — NOT between the loop plane and B. If the loop plane makes angle φ with B, then θ = 90° − φ. At maximum torque (B in plane), φ = 0°, so θ = 90°, sin90° = 1 → maximum. This trips up most students.
Mistake 2: Saying net force = 0 means no effect ▼
Net force = 0 means no translational acceleration (loop doesn't fly away). But net torque ≠ 0 means there IS rotational effect. The loop spins in place. This is exactly how an electric motor works.
Mistake 3: Forgetting N (number of turns) ▼
The formula for a single loop is τ = BIA sinθ. For a coil of N turns, every turn contributes, so τ = NIAB sinθ. In NCERT problems, "coil of 50 turns" means N=50. Always check if N is given.
Mistake 4: Confusing stable and unstable equilibrium ▼
At θ=0°: m is parallel to B. Torque = 0. If disturbed slightly, torque brings it back → STABLE. At θ=180°: m is anti-parallel to B. Torque = 0. But any small disturbance causes torque that pushes it further away → UNSTABLE. Think of a pencil balanced on its tip (unstable) vs lying flat (stable).
