Introduction

What is a Moving Coil Galvanometer?

A Moving Coil Galvanometer (MCG) is a sensitive electromagnetic instrument used for detecting and measuring small electric currents. It works on the principle that a current-carrying coil placed in a magnetic field experiences a torque.

τ
Deflecting Torque
τ = NIAB (radial field)
K
Restoring Torque
Kθ from phosphor bronze spring
θ
Deflection
θ = (NBA/K)·I ∝ I
B
Radial B field
sinθ always = 1
Key insight: The cylindrical soft iron core makes the field radial — so sinθ = 1 always. This means deflection θ is linearly proportional to current I. Without radial field, sinθ would change with angle making the scale non-linear!

Physics Behind It

Working Principle

1
Current enters the coil

When current I flows through the N-turn coil of area A, each turn experiences a magnetic force from the field B.

2
Deflecting torque is created

The magnetic torque on the coil is τ = NIAB sinθ. But since the field is radial by design, the plane of the coil is always parallel to B, so sinθ = 1 always.

Deflecting Torque (radial field)
τ_def = NIAB
This is always maximum because sinθ = 1 due to radial field. No angle dependence!
3
Restoring torque from spring

The phosphor bronze spring resists the rotation. As the coil deflects by angle θ, the spring provides restoring torque:

Restoring Torque (from spring)
τ_rest = Kθ
K = torsional constant of the spring (restoring torque per unit twist)
4
Equilibrium → deflection proportional to current

At steady state: deflecting torque = restoring torque

At equilibrium: NIAB = Kθ
θ = (NBA/K) · I
θ ∝ I → deflection is directly proportional to current → linear scale! This is the key result.

Sensitivity Analysis

Current Sensitivity & Voltage Sensitivity

Current Sensitivity (Iₛ)

Current sensitivity = deflection produced per unit current flowing through it.

Current Sensitivity
Iₛ = θ/I = NBA/K
Unit: rad/A or div/A. To increase Iₛ: increase N, B, A or decrease K (softer spring).

Voltage Sensitivity (Vₛ)

Voltage sensitivity = deflection per unit voltage across it.

Voltage Sensitivity
Vₛ = θ/V = NBA/KR = Iₛ/R
Unit: rad/V or div/V. Vₛ = Iₛ/R. Note: increasing N doubles Iₛ but also doubles R → Vₛ unchanged!
Important exam trap: Increasing current sensitivity does NOT always increase voltage sensitivity. Doubling N → doubles Iₛ, but R also doubles → Vₛ = Iₛ/R stays same. To increase Vₛ: increase B or A, or decrease K without changing R.
Example 19: Increase current sensitivity by 50%, resistance doubled — find voltage sensitivity change ▼
Given: New I'ₛ = Iₛ + 50/100 × Iₛ = 1.5 Iₛ = 3Iₛ/2  |  New R' = 2R

Initial Vₛ = Iₛ/R
New V'ₛ = I'ₛ/R' = (3Iₛ/2)/(2R) = 3Iₛ/4R = (3/4)(Iₛ/R) = (3/4)Vₛ

∴ Voltage sensitivity decreases to 75% of original — it DECREASES by 25%!
Conversion

Converting Galvanometer to Ammeter

A galvanometer has high resistance and can only handle small current. To measure large current, connect a low resistance shunt S in parallel.

1
Why parallel? Why low resistance?

Most of the large current I bypasses through shunt S. Only a small safe current Ig passes through the galvanometer. S must be very small so it doesn't affect the circuit.

2
Voltage across both is equal (parallel connection)

Ig × G = (I − Ig) × S

Shunt Resistance Formula
S = (Ig × G) / (I − Ig)
Or equivalently: S = [Ig/(I−Ig)] × G
3
Effective resistance of the ammeter
Parallel combination (Ammeter resistance)
Rₚ = GS/(G+S)
Since S << G, Rₚ ≈ S which is very small. Ideal ammeter resistance = 0.

Conversion

Converting Galvanometer to Voltmeter

A voltmeter must have very high resistance so it draws negligible current and doesn't disturb the circuit. Connect a high resistance Rₛ in series with the galvanometer.

1
Why series? Why high resistance?

The series resistance limits current to full-scale deflection current Ig when maximum voltage V is applied. High resistance ensures the voltmeter draws negligible current.

2
Full-scale deflection condition

At max voltage V: V = Ig(G + Rₛ)

Series Resistance Formula
Rₛ = V/Ig − G
Rₛ is very large (>> G). Ideal voltmeter resistance = ∞

⚠️ Exam Mistakes

Mistake 1: Ignoring radial field significance ▼
Without radial field, the torque would be NIAB sinα where α changes as coil rotates. Scale would be non-linear (compressed near ends). Radial field ensures sinα=1 always → uniform, linear scale. The cylindrical iron core is what creates radial field.
Mistake 2: Thinking more N always increases voltage sensitivity ▼
Doubling N doubles Iₛ = NBA/K. BUT it also doubles resistance R (more wire). Vₛ = Iₛ/R = NBA/KR. If both N and R double, Vₛ stays same. To genuinely increase Vₛ: increase B or A, or decrease K independently of wire length.
Mistake 3: Shunt in series / Series R in parallel ▼
Ammeter: shunt (small S) in PARALLEL — diverts excess current. Voltmeter: high resistance Rₛ in SERIES — limits current to safe Ig. Getting this backwards is the most common mistake. Remember: A-mmeter (parallel), V-oltmeter (series).
Mistake 4: Ideal ammeter and voltmeter resistance ▼
Ideal ammeter resistance = 0 Ω (so no voltage drop, doesn't affect circuit). Ideal voltmeter resistance = ∞ Ω (draws zero current, doesn't affect circuit). In reality: ammeter has tiny S ≈ Rₚ, voltmeter has huge Rₛ+G.
