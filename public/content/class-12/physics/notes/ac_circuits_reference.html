AC Circuits: Phasors & Pure Components

CBSE Class 12 Physics Reference — Foundation for LCR Circuits

1 Phasor Diagrams — The Core Idea

A phasor is a rotating vector that represents a sinusoidally varying quantity (current or EMF). Here's the exact mechanism:

  • The phasor's length = peak value (I₀ or E₀)
  • The phasor rotates anticlockwise with angular velocity ω (always anticlockwise — this is a fixed convention, never clockwise)
  • The projection of the phasor on the Y-axis at any instant gives you the instantaneous value

Why projection on Y-axis specifically? Because our equations are written in sine form: I = I₀sin(ωt), E = E₀sin(ωt). If the phasor makes angle ωt with the X-axis (horizontal reference), then Y-projection = (length) × sin(ωt) = I₀sin(ωt), which matches the equation.

Blind spot #1

If a problem gives you a cosine form like I = I₀cos(ωt), your phasor diagram starts not from the X-axis, but rotated 90° ahead of it (i.e., starting at the Y-axis itself). Always convert: cos(ωt) = sin(ωt + π/2). A cosine phasor is just a sine phasor sitting 90° ahead.

Blind spot #2

Time t = 0 is when you "freeze" the phasor diagram and draw it as a snapshot. The diagram is NOT static physics — it's a snapshot of two rotating arrows at one instant, maintaining a fixed angle between them forever.

2 AC Through a Pure Resistor

Setup: E = E₀sin(ωt) applied across resistance R only.

By Ohm's law instantaneously: IR = E

→ I = E/R = (E₀/R) sin(ωt)

→ I = I₀sin(ωt), where I₀ = E₀/R

Result: Comparing I = I₀sin(ωt) and E = E₀sin(ωt) — both have the same phase (no phase angle, no lag, no lead). Current and voltage rise and fall together, hit zero together, peak together.

Phasor diagram: Both I₀ and E₀ phasors point in the exact same direction, overlapping.

Special / Extreme Cases

  • This is the ONLY component where R works identically for AC and DC.
  • Blind spot: Students sometimes think "in phase" means I₀ = E₀ numerically. Wrong — it only means the phase angle is zero. Magnitudes are related by R, they are NOT equal unless R = 1Ω.

3 AC Through a Pure Inductor

Setup: E = E₀sin(ωt) applied to a pure inductance L (zero resistance).

E − L(dI/dt) = 0

→ dI/dt = (E₀/L) sin(ωt)

Integrating: I = −(E₀/ωL)cos(ωt)

Since −cos(ωt) = sin(ωt − π/2):

I = I₀ sin(ωt − π/2) [where I₀ = E₀/ωL]

Result: Current lags the EMF by phase angle π/2 (90°). Equivalently, voltage leads current by 90°.

Inductive Reactance: XL = ωL = 2πνL. It is directly proportional to frequency (XL ∝ ν).

Case What happens Why
DC source (ν = 0) XL = 0 Inductor offers ZERO opposition to steady DC (acts like a wire)
Very high freq. AC XL → ∞ Inductor almost completely blocks current
Conclusion (Memorize) "Inductor passes DC and blocks AC of very high frequency"

Blind spot #3

Don't confuse "passes DC" with "DC has no effect." At ν=0, XL=0 doesn't mean the inductor disappears — it just means it offers no additional opposition once steady current is established. (It still opposes the initial build-up).

4 AC Through a Pure Capacitor

Setup: E = E₀sin(ωt) applied to a capacitor C only.

Charge q = CE₀sin(ωt)

I = dq/dt = CE₀ω cos(ωt) = (E₀/(1/ωC)) cos(ωt)

Since cos(ωt) = sin(ωt + π/2):

I = I₀ sin(ωt + π/2) [where I₀ = E₀/(1/ωC)]

Result: Current leads the EMF by phase angle π/2 (90°). Equivalently, voltage lags current by 90°. (Exact opposite of the inductor).

Capacitive Reactance: XC = 1/ωC = 1/(2πνC). It is inversely proportional to frequency (XC ∝ 1/ν).

Case What happens Why
DC source (ν = 0) XC = ∞ Capacitor offers infinite opposition to DC (open circuit)
Very high freq. AC XC → 0 Capacitor offers almost no opposition, current flows freely
Conclusion (Memorize) "Capacitor blocks DC and passes AC of high frequency"

Blind spot #4

L passes DC, blocks high-freq AC.
C blocks DC, passes high-freq AC.

Think of an inductor as "lazy to change" (passes steady DC easily) and a capacitor as "needs to keep charging/discharging" (DC can't cycle it, AC can).

CIVIL Mnemonic (Blind spot #5)

  • C I V : In a Capacitor, Current (I) leads Voltage (V).
  • V I L : Voltage (V) leads Current (I) in an Inductor (L).

6. Worked Examples

Ex 4: Resistor

R = 20Ω, E_rms = 110V, ν = 50Hz

  • I_rms = 110/20 = 5.5 A
  • I₀ = √2 × 5.5 = 7.78 A
  • At t=0.1s: I = I₀sin(π/4)...

Ex 5: Inductor

E = 220sin(100πt), L = 1/π H

  • X_L = 100π × (1/π) = 100Ω
  • I₀ = 220/100 = 2.2 A
  • I = 2.2 sin(100πt − π/2)
  • I_rms = 2.2/1.414 = 1.55 A

Ex 6: Capacitor

C = 10μF, E = 220sin(100πt)

  • X_C = 1/(100π×10μF) = 318.5Ω
  • I₀ = 220/318.5 = 0.691 A
  • I = 0.691 sin(100πt + π/2)
  • I_rms = 0.691/1.414 = 0.489 A

Why This Matters for LCR (Preview)

When R, L, C combine in one circuit, you cannot simply add the phase-shifted voltages arithmetically (V_R + V_L + V_C ≠ total V algebraically) because they're not in phase with each other. V_R is in phase with I, V_L leads I by 90°, and V_C lags I by 90°. You must add them as vectors (phasors) using the Pythagorean-style combination, which is exactly why we built the phasor foundation first. The next step is vector addition step by step!
