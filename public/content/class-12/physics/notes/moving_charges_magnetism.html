Magnetic field of a solenoid

A solenoid is a long coil of insulated wire wound in a helix. When current flows, it creates a strong, uniform magnetic field inside and essentially zero field outside.

Concept What is a solenoid?
  • Field inside is uniform and parallel to the axis
  • Field outside is assumed to be zero
  • Resembles a bar magnet — South (S) and North (N) poles at ends
  • Valid only when length l >> radius r
  • n = turns per unit length (not total turns N)
  • n = N / l
  • More turns per metre → stronger field
  • Larger current → stronger field
💡 Think of it as a tunnel of circular current loops. Inside the tunnel, fields from every loop point the same way and add up. Outside, they cancel out to nearly zero.
Derivation Using Ampere's Circuital Law

We choose a rectangular Amperian loop PQRS. Side PQ = L lies inside along the axis. We evaluate ∮ B·dl around this loop.

Sides QR and SP (perpendicular to axis): B is perpendicular to the path element dl at these sides.

B · dl = B dl cos90° = 0 → No contribution

Side RS (outside the solenoid): Magnetic field is approximately zero outside.

B ≈ 0 → No contribution from RS

Side PQ (inside, along axis): B is uniform and parallel to dl here (θ = 0°, cos0 = 1).

∮ B · dl = B × L ← only contribution

Number of turns in length L = n × L. Each carries current I, so total enclosed current:

I_net = n × L × I

Apply Ampere's law: ∮ B·dl = μ₀ × I_net

B × L = μ₀ × n × L × I

Cancel L from both sides — L disappears! This means B doesn't depend on which cross-section you're at inside.

B = μ₀ n I ← Final result
B = μ₀ n I
🔑 Key insight: B is independent of the position inside, the total length, and the diameter of the solenoid. Only n (turn density) and I matter.

Force on a moving charge in a magnetic field

When a charged particle moves through a magnetic field B, it experiences a force that is always perpendicular to both its velocity and the field.

Formula Magnetic force on a moving charge
F = q(v × B) = qvB sin θ

Force is ZERO when:

  • Particle at rest (v = 0)
  • v ∥ B (θ = 0°, sin 0° = 0)
  • v anti-parallel to B (θ = 180°)

Force is MAXIMUM when:

  • v ⊥ B (θ = 90°, sin 90° = 1)
  • F_max = qvB
  • This is the circular motion case
⚡ Since F is always ⊥ to v, the magnetic force does NO work. So it cannot change the speed or kinetic energy of the particle — only its direction!
Direction Right-hand thumb rule / cross product

For F = q(v × B): Point fingers along v, curl toward B → thumb points in direction of F (for +q).

Derivation Force on a current-carrying conductor: F = BIl sin θ

A current-carrying wire in a magnetic field feels a force because of the drift motion of electrons inside it.

Let: n = free electrons per unit volume, A = cross-section area, l = length of conductor in field, v_d = drift velocity of electrons, e = electron charge.

Force on each single electron moving with drift velocity v_d in field B at angle θ:

F' = e v_d B sin θ

Total number of free electrons in the conductor of length l and cross-section A:

N = n × A × l

Total force on all electrons = force on the conductor (Newton's 3rd law — electrons push the lattice):

F = F' × N = (e v_d B sin θ) × (n A l) F = (n e A v_d) × B l sin θ

Recognize that current I = n e A v_d (standard relation between current and drift velocity):

F = I B l sin θ
F = BIl sin θ or F = I (l × B)
🔬 Physical meaning: The force on a wire is just the combined magnetic push on all the drifting electrons inside it. Elegant — no new physics needed, just counting electrons!

Circular & helical motion in magnetic field

When a charged particle enters a magnetic field perpendicular to it, the magnetic force acts as centripetal force, curving the path into a perfect circle. A component parallel to B creates helical motion.

Derivation Radius of circular orbit — step by step
Core idea: Magnetic force (perpendicular to v) provides the centripetal force for circular motion. Set them equal and solve.

For a particle of mass m moving in a circle of radius r at speed v_⊥, the required centripetal force is:

F_centripetal = mv_⊥² / r

When v ⊥ B (θ = 90°), the magnetic force is at its maximum value:

F_magnetic = qv_⊥B (since sin 90° = 1)

The magnetic force IS the centripetal force — set them equal:

mv_⊥² / r = qv_⊥B

Cancel one v_⊥ from both sides (v_⊥ ≠ 0), then rearrange for r:

mv_⊥ / r = qB r = mv_⊥ / (qB) = p / (qB)
r = mv_⊥ / (qB) = p / (qB)
Bigger r → heavier particle (larger m), faster speed (larger v), weaker field (smaller B)
Smaller r → more charge (larger q), stronger field (larger B) — tighter spiral
Derivation Time period T — and why it doesn't depend on speed

The particle covers one full circumference (2πr) at speed v_⊥ in time T:

T = 2πr / v_⊥

Substitute r = mv_⊥/(qB) from the radius formula:

T = (2π / v_⊥) × (mv_⊥ / qB)

The v_⊥ terms cancel completely — remarkable!

T = 2πm / (qB) ← velocity has vanished!
T = 2πm / (qB) → independent of velocity!
Frequency: f = qB / (2πm)
Angular freq: ω = qB / m
🌀 This is the cyclotron principle! A faster particle travels a bigger circle, but always completes one revolution in the same time T. This is why cyclotron resonance works.
Concept Helical motion & pitch

If velocity is NOT perpendicular to B, decompose it into two parts:

v_⊥ (perpendicular to B)
→ Creates circular motion in the plane ⊥ to B
→ r = mv_⊥/(qB)
→ Changed by magnetic force
v_∥ (parallel to B)
→ Unaffected by B (F = q v_∥ × B = 0)
→ Particle drifts along B axis unchanged
→ Creates the "forward" motion
Pitch P = v_∥ × T = v_∥ × (2πm / qB)
Think of it as a corkscrew: the particle spins in a circle while simultaneously moving forward. Pitch = distance per full spin.

Example 11 (Textbook) — Proton beam at 60° to B field

v = 4×10⁵ m/s, angle = 60° with B = 0.3 T

v_⊥ = 4×10⁵ × sin60° = 4×10⁵ × (√3/2) = 2√3 × 10⁵ m/s
v_∥ = 4×10⁵ × cos60° = 4×10⁵ × 0.5 = 2×10⁵ m/s

r = mv_⊥/(qB) = (1.67×10⁻²⁷ × 2√3×10⁵) / (1.6×10⁻¹⁹ × 0.3) ≈ 0.012 m

T = 2πr / v_⊥ = (2 × 3.14 × 0.012) / (2√3 × 10⁵)

Pitch = v_∥ × T = 2×10⁵ × T = 0.044 m = 4.4 cm

Example 10 (Textbook) — Proton vs alpha particle radius comparison

Both accelerated through same potential V, then enter same B field perpendicular.

Radius r = mv/(qB) = √(2mK) / (qB) where K = kinetic energy = qV

So r = √(2mqV) / (qB) = √(2mV/q) / B

For proton: m_p = m, q_p = e
For α-particle: m_α = 4m, q_α = 2e

r_p / r_α = √(m/e) / √(4m/2e) = √(m/e) / √(2m/e) = 1/√2

Lorentz force — electric + magnetic combined

In real situations a particle can experience both an electric field E and a magnetic field B simultaneously. The total force is the Lorentz force.

Formula The Lorentz force
F_Lorentz = F_Electric + F_Magnetic = qE + q(v × B)
F_Lorentz = q [E + (v × B)]

Electric component qE:

  • Acts on stationary or moving charge
  • Directed along E (or opposite for −q)
  • Does work → changes kinetic energy
  • Changes both direction and speed

Magnetic component q(v×B):

  • Acts only on a moving charge
  • Always perpendicular to v
  • Does NO work → KE unchanged
  • Changes direction only, not speed
For a negative charge: the force direction is opposite to that for a positive charge. The sign of q in the formula handles this automatically.

⚡ Velocity Selector Condition met! qE ≈ qvB → forces cancel → E = vB → only particles with v = E/B pass through undeflected. This is how a velocity selector works!
Application Velocity selector

When both E and B fields are present and perpendicular to each other and to v:

Electric force on the particle (upward, say):

F_E = qE (upward)

Magnetic force on the particle moving right through B (downward by right-hand rule):

F_M = qvB (downward)

For the particle to pass through undeflected, forces must balance:

qE = qvB

Cancel q from both sides — the charge drops out! Selected velocity:

v = E / B
v_selected = E / B (independent of q and m!)
🔬 Particles of any mass or charge that have exactly this velocity pass straight through. All others deflect. This is how mass spectrometers select particles of a specific speed!
